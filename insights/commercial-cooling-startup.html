Starting Up Commercial Cooling the Right Way

April 28, 2025 5 min read

Whether they sit on a standalone building, a strip center, or a sprawling facility, commercial rooftop units handle the cooling and ventilation your operation depends on — and they take a beating across the seasons. As spring turns to summer, a proper cooling startup is what separates equipment that runs all season from equipment that fails on the first hot afternoon.

What a commercial cooling startup involves

A cooling startup is a focused planned maintenance procedure that gets rooftop units ready for the demands of summer before the heat arrives. It’s detailed work, and it’s best handled by experienced commercial HVAC technicians who do it the same way every time. When FirstCall Mechanical performs a startup, our own technicians work through a consistent scope that includes:

  • Replacing air filters
  • Inspecting condenser coils, belts, drains, fan blades, and motors
  • Checking refrigerant pressures and testing for leaks
  • Inspecting and cleaning electrical connections
  • Verifying amp draw against manufacturer specifications
  • Measuring supply and return air temperatures

That last group of checks matters as much as the visible work. Catching a marginal electrical connection or a low refrigerant charge in spring is the difference between a quick correction and a mid-July emergency. For how this fits a year-round program, see our guide to commercial HVAC planned maintenance.

Proactive belt changes

Belts are deceptively important: without functioning fan belts, there is no air conditioning. They should be checked at every maintenance visit and proactively replaced during the spring-to-summer transition. Over a season, belts loosen, wear, and crack. Replacing them on a schedule — rather than after they snap — cuts down on repeated tightening, protects the unit from added strain, and removes one of the most common causes of a no-cooling call.

Condenser coil chemical cleanings

Condenser coils collect dirt, debris, and seasonal nuisances like cottonwood that choke airflow and drag down efficiency, operating pressure, and cooling capacity. A garden-hose rinse rarely reaches the buildup packed into the tight spaces between coil fins. A proactive chemical cleaning clears that material out and restores the unit to the performance it was designed for — which directly reduces the energy it burns trying to keep up.

Why timing matters: startup work done in spring, before peak load, is fast and low-disruption. The same issues caught in July mean after-hours rates, expedited parts, and tenants or customers sweating while you wait.

What skipping the startup actually costs

When a unit is pushed into summer without a proper startup, the failures are rarely random. A loose belt slips and the unit stops cooling on the first 95-degree day. A coil caked with cottonwood forces the compressor to run hotter and longer, spiking the energy bill and shortening the equipment’s life. A small refrigerant leak that would have taken minutes to find in April becomes a low-charge, iced-up coil and an emergency dispatch in August. Each of these is a predictable, preventable outcome — and each one costs far more reactively than it would have during a scheduled spring visit. The startup isn’t an optional tune-up; it’s the cheapest insurance you can buy against a season of breakdowns.

A consistent scope across every location

For organizations running cooling at more than one site, consistency is everything. When the same self-performing team executes an identical startup scope at every building, you avoid the uneven quality and improvised technique that creep in when each location is handled by a different contractor. Because FirstCall Mechanical self-performs this work with its own technicians, the startup on your busiest facility is performed to the same standard as the one on your smallest — no hand-offs, no guesswork.

  • Proactive belt changes prevent the most common no-cooling failures.
