Choosing a Commercial HVAC Service Company: 13 Questions to Ask

May 16, 2025 6 min read

Choosing who maintains and repairs your commercial HVAC is one of the most consequential decisions a facilities team makes. Get it right and you save time, headaches, and a great deal of money. Get it wrong and you inherit every one of those problems. These thirteen questions help you separate a true self-performing partner from a middleman who simply passes your work along.

Why the right questions matter

Thirteen is supposed to be an unlucky number. In commercial HVAC, it can be the opposite — a checklist of thirteen questions that surfaces exactly what you need to know before signing. The single most important thing to confirm is whether a company self-performs the work with its own technicians or coordinates outside vendors. That one answer shapes accountability, quality, and cost across every site you operate.

Experience, track record, and references

Start with whether the company can actually do the work, consistently, at scale:

  • 1. How long have you been in business? Look for depth and breadth of experience measured in years — decades, ideally. Newer firms can do fine work, but proven longevity matters when your operation depends on equipment running.
  • 2. Who are your current customers? Ask about the building types they serve (retail, office, industrial, multifamily), the number and size of locations, and their geographic footprint.
  • 3. Can you provide references I can call? A confident partner hands over references in similar facilities without hesitation.
  • 4. Are your technicians employees or subcontractors? This is the question that matters most. FirstCall Mechanical self-performs with its own trained technicians, so the people on your roof answer to the company that signed your agreement.

Scope, equipment, and self-performance

Next, confirm the company can cover the full lifecycle of your equipment — not just the easy calls:

  • 5. What services do you provide? The strongest partners cover the full range: planned maintenance, troubleshooting, repair, and replacement under one roof.
  • 6. Do you have experience with my specific equipment? Your rooftop units, chillers, and controls should be familiar territory, not a learning curve billed to you.
  • 7. Do you self-perform across all trades? FirstCall self-performs commercial HVAC, building controls, plumbing, and electrical — so coordination happens inside one company instead of across a vendor chain.
  • 8. How do you handle planned maintenance? A real program is built to your equipment and runtime. Our approach to planned maintenance sets cadence by risk, not a generic calendar.
  • 9. What is your emergency response time? Ask for committed response windows and how after-hours calls are staffed.
Watch for the middleman: if a company can’t tell you who will physically be on site or describes itself as “coordinating” service, you may be hiring a layer of management rather than the technicians who do the work. Self-performance removes that layer.

Reporting, pricing, and accountability

Finally, make sure you’ll have visibility and predictable costs across your portfolio:

  • 10. What reporting and documentation do you provide? You should get an equipment list, service history, and work-order tracking for every unit at every site.
  • 11. How is pricing structured? Look for a uniform scope with clearly defined costs and timelines, applied consistently across locations.
  • 12. How do you maintain consistency across multiple sites? Standardized schedules and one accountable team keep every building to the same standard.
  • 13. Who owns the outcome when something goes wrong? With a self-performing partner, the answer is simple — one company, no finger-pointing.

Key takeaways

  • Confirm whether a company self-performs with its own technicians or coordinates outside vendors — it shapes everything.
  • Vet experience, references, and proven work in facilities like yours.
  • Demand full-lifecycle scope: planned maintenance, repair, and replacement under one roof.
  • Insist on consistent reporting, uniform pricing, and single-source accountability across every site.
